The Central Limit Theorem underpins alot of statistics and modelling. Once you understand it, what it does and what it doesn’t, critically evaluating the models and methods you use becomes easier.

When do people use them, it’s just a theory, right?

First let’s start with a couple of situations where you may be using the central limit theorem without even knowing it:

* Have you ever done any A/B testing? Have you ever tested the hypothesis that two means, proportions or variances are equal? These tests typically use the CLT to achieve the results they depend on.
* Have you ever run a regression model of some kind? A generalised linear model? Logistic regression? Our understanding of how these models are acting - our understanding of the uncertainty these models are able to mitigate- is based on the CLT.
* What about ensemble or model averaging methods? Guess which 'trick' they're using?
* Confidence intrvals? Yep, those too.

What isn’t the central limit theorem?

The most common misperception we hear is the idea that “when sample sizes get big, all distributions go to normal.” Put simply, if this was the case most statisticians could all take up new hobbies. We’ll show you why this isn’t the case.

So what is the central limit theorem.

So it’s central limit theorems all the way down. That’s right, Central Limit Theorems. There’s a whole collection of them, each relating to a similar but slightly different situation.
